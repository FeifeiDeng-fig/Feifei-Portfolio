Wyze Air.

Creating a smarter IoT experience that made the mobile app an essential companion to the device — not an afterthought.

Sole Product Designer
PM, Dev team
May 2021 – 2022
Mobile (iOS & Android)

Context

Wyze Air Purifier is part of the Wyze IoT ecosystem. It uses rule engines, multiple filter types, and real-time air quality data to help users respond proactively to indoor and outdoor changes.

The problem: 70% of smart air purifier users never connect their device to the mobile app. Our goal was to make the app connection feel valuable — not just another remote control, but a layer of intelligence the device alone can't provide.

Approach

As the sole designer, I owned the full experience from research through handoff. I started with a user survey to understand behaviors around smart air purifiers, then built user groups and use cases to define what the app should uniquely offer.

I established design principles early and shared them with the PM — this aligned expectations and gave us a shared language for trade-off conversations throughout the project. From there, I created information architecture and key user flows before moving into design.

The app needed to offer something the physical device couldn't — contextual intelligence about air quality and automation that responds proactively to the environment.

Key decisions

Prioritizing features through use cases

Rather than building everything at once, I used user flows as a communication tool with PM to refine the PRD, identify integration dependencies, and divide features into phased releases that fit the timeline.

Design principles as alignment tool

Establishing principles early helped set realistic expectations with the PM and kept iterations focused. When debates arose about scope, we could point back to shared principles rather than personal preferences.

Testing with real constraints

I conducted 5 moderated usability tests using the beta app — not polished prototypes. This revealed real friction points like setup confusion and settings discoverability that a prototype couldn't surface.

Impact

The product page launched through beta testing and continued iterating. The design covered setup, main controls, settings, and key automation experiences — all grounded in research and refined through cross-functional collaboration.

This project shaped how I think about IoT design: the best smart experiences aren't about giving users more control — they're about reducing the need for control in the first place.
